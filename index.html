Time-Controllability Benchmark for Music Generation

Can today's music-generation models place a musical event at the exact moment you ask for? A reproducible benchmark of temporal instruction-following — by Taan.ai.

1 · Motivation

Taan.ai builds time-controllable music generation conditioned on video. A film cut, a beat drop on an action moment, silence before a reveal — music for video lives and dies by when things happen. Our customers' core requirement is not "generate nice music"; it is "make the drums enter at second 6" — and have that actually happen at second 6.

While building our beta with 21 agencies, we found that leading music-generation APIs follow what to play far better than when to play it. No public benchmark measured this, so we built one. This page presents the benchmark, the measurement pipeline, and results across four state-of-the-art systems — plus an early read on Taan's own engine.

2 · What the benchmark measures

250timed prompts
~500events scored per model (2 generations × 250 prompts)
4commercial systems compared
±44 msmeasurement error (median, vs human labels)

Each prompt embeds explicit temporal instructions into a normal music brief, across three types:

  • Type 1 one timed event — "bring in drums at exactly the 6th second", "cut all percussion at the 18th second", "silence until the 5th second"
  • Type 2 a timed sequence — "silence until 3 s, drums at 6 s, brass at 15 s"
  • Type 3 global tempo — "exactly 120 BPM"

Events span five instrument families (percussion, bass, harmonic/melodic layers, strings, silence/full-mix). We score category adherence, not timbre quality: "a cello melody at 16 s" is credited when a new sustained tonal layer enters at 16 s — the benchmark isolates timing, not audio quality.

Two-tier scoring — separating “wrong time” from “didn’t do it”

  1. On-time rate — the event is detected within a tolerance ladder of the instructed instant: ±250 ms / ±500 ms (headline) / ±1 s / ±2 s.
  2. Realized rate (±5 s) — the event clearly happens near the asked time at all. The gap between the two tiers is the timing-control deficit, distinct from the capability deficit.

Comparative read

  • Lyria — best strict timing (22.3% ≤500 ms) and best BPM; strongest on melodic entries.
  • ElevenLabs v2 — best overall instruction-follower (realized 71.6%, best ≤1 s/≤2 s, best duration); a clear, measurable upgrade over v1 on every timing column.
  • Suno — competitive event realization, weakest fine timing, does not respect duration.
  • Ranking is tolerance-dependent — we publish the full ladder rather than a single number.

On strict ≤500 ms timing — the metric that matters for video — early Taan outputs already exceed every commercial system (29% vs 15–22%), including a drums entry landed 14 ms from the instructed second. Where Taan missed, the event typically occurred but early — a controllability bug we can fix at the score level, unlike API models where timing is not addressable at all. The measurement pipeline is level-invariant by construction, so Taan's quieter, unmastered audio is scored on equal footing (verified: identical detections under −12/−20 dB gain).

5 · How we measure — the event-detection pipeline

prompt → expected events
instrument, time, entry/exit→ routing
each event → best feature source→ features
neural stems · HPSS · onset trains · timbre novelty→ 3 conditions
transition ∧ precondition ∧ persistence→ two-tier score

Key design choices

  • Per-family routing. Named drums → the drum stem of a neural source separator; broad percussion → harmonic/percussive separation ∪ drum-stem onsets; bass → bass stem (+ sub-band fallback); new melodic layers (cello, strings, brass…) → a timbre-novelty detector (MFCC + chroma self-similarity), since no separator isolates "the new melody" from an existing mix; silence → full-mix loudness vs a clip-relative floor.
  • Percussion is an onset train, not a level — we detect where the train of percussive accents starts/stops, then snap to the exact accent. Robust to sparse grooves and to a new rhythm layered over an existing one.
  • Three agreements per event. A detection requires (a) the strongest matching transition near the instructed time, (b) the correct precondition (absent-before for entries / absent-after for exits), and (c) persistence — the instrument must stay after entering (a stray hit is not an entry; a musical rest is not an ending). This is what controls false positives.
  • Level-invariance. Every threshold is relative to the clip's own measured levels — no recalibration across providers, masters, or our own unmastered outputs.
  • One frozen configuration for all models; every constant documented with its rationale.

Validation — the instrument's own error bar

  • Human-labelled gold set (30 generated clips, onsets labelled to ±15–20 ms): 45/45 labelled transitions detected, median error 44 ms, 38/41 within 500 ms; 5/7 instruction-ignored negatives correctly rejected (the 2 misses are documented category-blindness of the melody detector).
  • Blind held-out test (new clips, detector given no times): correct on clean entries/exits; failures found there (musical rests mistaken for endings, simultaneous swap events) were fixed and re-frozen before the full run.
  • Detector error (44 ms median) is ~10× smaller than the tightest tolerance we report (500 ms).

Known limitations

  • The melody detector is category-blind: it detects "a new tonal layer", not "specifically a cello" — quantified as 5/7 negative rejection and published with the results.
  • Swells/crescendos have no single onset; detector and ear can disagree by up to ~1 s on gradual entries.
  • Timing is scored on category-level events; instrument identity and audio quality are explicitly out of scope.
