Time-Controllability Benchmark for Music Generation

Can today's music-generation models place a musical event at the exact moment you ask for? A reproducible benchmark of temporal instruction-following - by taan.ai.

1 · Motivation

taan.ai is building time-controllable music generation conditioned on video. A film cut, a beat drop on an action moment, silence before a reveal, music for video lives and dies by when things happen. Our customers' core requirement is to generate music that aligns with their videos and has a significant impact. We understand the customers' requests such as "make the drums enter at second 6" which are temporally specific, and we want to hit perfectly at those impact points.

While building our beta with 21 agencies, we found that leading music-generation APIs follow what to play far better than when to play it. No public benchmark measured this, so we built one. This page presents the benchmark, the overview of measurement pipeline, and results across four SotA systems, plus an early prototypical result on taan's own engine.

2 · What the benchmark measures

250timed prompts
~500events scored per model (2 generations × 250 prompts)
4commercial systems compared
±42 msmeasurement error (median, vs human annotated labels)

Each prompt embeds explicit temporal instructions into a normal music brief, across three types:

  • Type 1 one timed event — "bring in drums at exactly the 6th second", "cut all percussion at the 18th second", "silence until the 5th second"
  • Type 2 a timed sequence — "silence until 3 s, drums at 6 s, brass at 15 s"
  • Type 3 global tempo — "exactly 120 BPM"

Events span five instrument families (percussion, bass, harmonic/melodic layers, strings, silence/full-mix). We score category adherence, not timbre quality: "a cello melody at 16 s" is credited when a new sustained tonal layer enters at 16 s. The benchmark isolates timing, not audio quality.

Two-tier scoring — separating “wrong time” from “didn’t do it”

  1. On-time rate — the event is detected within a tolerance ladder of the instructed instant: ±250 ms / ±500 ms (headline) / ±1 s / ±2 s.
  2. Realized rate (±5 s) — the event clearly happens near the asked time at all. The gap between the two tiers is the timing-control deficit, distinct from the capability deficit.

Comparative read

  • ElevenLabs v2: best overall timing controller: leads ≤500 ms (18.0%), ≤1 s, ≤2 s and event realization (60.9%), plus the best duration compliance. A clear, measurable upgrade over v1 across the whole timing ladder.
  • Lyria: sharpest at the very tightest tolerance (best ≤250 ms) and best global tempo; strongest on string and melodic entries.
  • Suno: weakest fine timing and ignores the requested duration.
  • Ranking is tolerance-dependent. We publish the full ladder rather than a single number.

4 · Our prototype results, taan engine

We scored an early batch of Taan's own outputs. Through the identical evaluation pipeline. The engine is at an early, prototypical stage, so rather than an aggregate over a tiny sample we show three prototypical results, events taan was asked to place, and placed, on the requested second:

InstructionAsked atLanded atError
Bring in the drums6.0 s6.014 s14 ms
Enter a deep bass12.0 s11.958 s42 ms
Hold silence, then start the music5.0 s4.783 s217 ms

All three land within 250 ms of the instructed instant — two of them within ~40 ms — a tolerance at which the best commercial system clears under 9% of its events. These are prototypical proof points, not an aggregate score: the engine is early and not every prompt is tested yet. The point is that when taan places an event, it can place it to the frame — the controllability that current SotA models structurally cannot offer. As we scale the model and the benchmark together, we expect this precision to carry into the full validation.

5 · How we measure — the event-detection pipeline

prompt → expected events
instrument, time, entry/exit→ routing

Validation — the instrument's own error bar

  • Human-labelled gold set (30 generated clips, onsets labelled to ±15–20 ms): 44/45 labelled transitions detected, median error 42 ms, 37/44 within 500 ms.
  • Blind held-out test (new clips, detector given no times): correct on clean entries/exits; failures found there (musical rests mistaken for endings, simultaneous swap events) were fixed and re-frozen before the full run.
  • Detector error (42 ms median) is ~10× smaller than the tightest tolerance we report (500 ms).

Known limitations

  • Swells/crescendos have no single onset; detector and ear can disagree by up to ~1 s on gradual entries.
  • Timing is scored on category-level events; exact instrument identity and audio quality are kept explicitly out of scope.
